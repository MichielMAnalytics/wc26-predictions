How these predictions were made

This chart is filled in by a statistical model, not by hand. Here's the reasoning behind it.

  1. Team strength. Every men's international from 2018–2026 (~8,000 matches) feeds a World-Football Elo rating — weighting World Cups above friendlies and bigger wins more. Strongest right now: Spain, Argentina, France, England, Brazil.
  2. Scorelines. A Dixon-Coles goals model turns those strengths into the probability of every exact scoreline for each fixture (recent and important games count more), including home advantage for the hosts (USA / Canada / Mexico).
  3. Who's in form / who's hurt. Current injuries, suspensions, coach changes and momentum were researched for all 48 squads and nudge each team up or down — e.g. Brazil are marked down without Rodrygo, Militão, Estêvão (and a doubtful Neymar); rising, healthy Morocco get a small lift.
  4. The betting market. Vig-free bookmaker odds are blended in. This is a real correction: on its own the model underrated France & England and overrated Morocco & Japan; the market pulls them back toward consensus.
  5. Pool-optimal picks. Each score is chosen to maximise expected Scorito points (you get points for the right result, plus a bonus for the exact score). That's why you'll see lots of 1-0 / 0-1: getting the result right is worth the most, and a one-goal win for the favourite is the single most-likely exact score.

Why Spain to win? The model and the betting market independently agree Spain is the most likely champion (~15%), just ahead of Argentina, France and England — so it's the safest title pick.

Does it actually work? Tested on matches it had never seen, it's about 26% more accurate than a naïve baseline, calls the correct result ~60% of the time, and beats both a plain Elo model and an "always 1-1" strategy. Caveat: tournaments are high-variance — upsets (hello, 2022) happen, so treat deep runs as likelihoods, not certainties.
